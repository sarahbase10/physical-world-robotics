Research Brief · May 2026
Confidential · Base10 LP Eyes

Humanoids will take longer than the market thinks — and the near-term trade is task-specific automation.

The dominant narrative says generalist humanoid robots are an imminent labor-replacement wave. Our primary research with the leading OEMs and policy-model labs disagrees — closing the 90% to 95–98% reliability gap that enterprise deployment requires looks qualitatively different from the data-and-scale problem capital is currently pricing.

The opportunity now is the enabling software and infrastructure layer underneath domain-specific robotic automation, with manufacturing as the most immediate adopter.

The Thesis · Six Points

What we believe — in the time it takes to finish a coffee

A condensed read on where physical-world robotics actually is, what investors are mispricing, and the layers where venture-scale outcomes most plausibly compound.

01

Hardware is no longer the bottleneck.

The model layer is.

Bipedal walking, climbing, and balance have been solved hardware problems since Boston Dynamics' Petman. The gating constraints today are generalization across unstructured environments and dexterous manipulation — both software-first.

02

The 90% → 98% gap is not a data-scaling problem.

Reliability bar

Transformer-based VLAs reach 90% task reliability cheaply. Closing the remaining gap to enterprise-grade reliability appears to require architectural and physics-modeling breakthroughs not on a predictable curve.

03

The transformer moment for robotics has not arrived.

Model scale

The largest open-source VLA today is ~7B parameters — two orders of magnitude below frontier LLMs while spanning more modalities. A 10-year arc to broad generalization is plausible. Capital is pricing 3–5.

04

Manufacturing is the near-term wedge.

Not households

Bounded variability, discrete tasks, and acute labor pressure mean domain-specific AI arms can step-function past ABB / Fanuc / Kuka without ever needing full generalization. That is the deployable opportunity for the next 5–10 years.

05

We invest the picks & shovels.

Not the bodies

Our exposure compounds whether or not any individual humanoid OEM wins: data pipelines, robotics DevOps, manufacturing-specific training data, and workflow orchestration. Value accrues from automation broadly, not humanoids specifically.

06

Self-driving 2016, not LLMs 2022.

Right analogy

Total deployed humanoids in the US today are under ~300 across all OEMs, almost entirely single-site pilots. That is the Waymo-Pacifica era. The category is real; the timing consensus is wrong.

Differential Theses

Four ways to invest robotics without underwriting humanoids

Each thesis sits at a layer that compounds whether or not any individual OEM wins. We weight the picks-and-shovels theses higher today given our timing view on generalist humanoids.

Thesis 01 Highest demand today

Data-to-Deployment Flywheel

Egocentric & teleop data providers — applied to domain-specific data scarcity at frontier AI labs and humanoid OEMs — produce venture-scale outcomes because data coverage compounds: better policies → more deployments → more edge cases → lower marginal cost of capability.

Why it works

General ego-data is being commoditized, but no one delivers it at true scale. Whoever can reliably produce tens of thousands of hours per quarter wins the market.

  • Physical Intelligence is buying ~40k hrs/quarter of egocentric data at $25/hr.
  • Skild is buying ~10k hrs/quarter at the same rate.
  • Labs are sourcing from every available supplier, including 3-FTE startups. Pricing is unscientific. Demand is unbounded.
Expansion path. Narrow data wedge → labeling + QA + evaluation → fine-tuned policies / skill libraries → monitoring + retraining → an "embodied model-ops" layer.
Thesis 02 Most defensible near-term

Manufacturing Data Infrastructure as Picks & Shovels

Manufacturing-specific training data infrastructure — applied to domain-specific data scarcity at manufacturing operators and manufacturing-focused robotics OEMs — produces venture-scale outcomes because the relevant training data is structurally different from humanoid ego data.

Why it works

Training data for manufacturing robots is capturable without a deployed robot, lives in bounded domains, and has no scalable pipeline today. The buyer (plant ops) has budget, urgency, and a near-term ROI case.

  • Fixed-perspective video, force/torque, 3D point cloud, and process logs all capturable from human operators on existing lines.
  • Domain-specific models tolerate part variability — the exact failure mode that has capped classical robotics for 60 years.
Expansion path. One vertical's data pipeline (auto or electronics assembly) → labeling/QA/eval infra → simulation + synthetic data → default training data layer for manufacturing robotics.
Thesis 03 Portfolio exposure

Robotics DevOps as Picks & Shovels

Robotics data & DevOps infrastructure — applied to deployment reliability and operations at humanoid OEMs and enterprise operators — produces venture-scale outcomes because every deployed fleet creates persistent demand for observability, evaluation, regression testing, and safe rollout.

Why it works

Every OEM is currently rebuilding the same plumbing. As pilots become fleets, the operational question shifts from "can it do the task once?" to "can it do it 10,000 times safely?" A Datadog + GitHub Actions + feature-flags layer for multimodal data.

  • Skild took ~2 years to build their feedback loop with Google's engineering resources.
  • Apptronik spent >1 year on internal data infrastructure despite engineering support from Google.
  • Agility flagged iteration cycles as their #1 bottleneck.
Expansion path. Data tooling → eval + simulation pipelines → fleet monitoring → policy marketplace / skill distribution. Cross-robot compatibility is the platform moat.
Thesis 04 Portfolio exposure

Workflow Control Plane for Physical Operations

Workflow orchestration — applied to integration into real workflows at enterprise operators — produces venture-scale outcomes because robots only create value when embedded in real operations. Software that translates enterprise processes into robotic tasks (across heterogeneous fleets) becomes the control plane for physical operations.

Why it works

WMS, MES, and ERP systems were designed for humans and deterministic machines, not probabilistic AI. Today, orchestration is bespoke integration work by SIs — brittle, expensive, and per-deployment. A dedicated orchestration platform standardizes that layer and enables multi-robot interop.

Expansion path. One robot type, one workflow (e.g., warehouse picking or factory line-side logistics) → heterogeneous fleets → deeper SoR integration → operational control plane for physical labor automation across facilities.

Key Debates

What would change our minds

1

When does our humanoid stance become falsifiable?

Buyer signal

The signal we are watching is direct buyer feedback from active pilot operators — Mercedes-Benz, BMW, Hyundai, GXO — on whether reliability is moving past the 95% threshold across more than one task class on more than one robot. We are building those relationships now. Public announcements alone are not enough; pilot extensions and multi-site rollouts are.

2

What if humanoids need architectural breakthroughs?

Bull case for our exposure

If generalization requires more than data scaling, the manufacturing opportunity gets longer, not shorter — domain-specific models become the durable form factor for the next decade rather than a stepping stone. That outcome strengthens theses 02–04, where our portfolio exposure already lives.

Posture

How we are deploying

We are net-cautious on humanoid OEMs and net-constructive on the layers underneath. That posture lines up against four investable wedges — data pipelines, manufacturing training data, robotics DevOps, and workflow orchestration — and two existing portfolio positions (Destro AI, Soma Stacks) that already express the thesis. We expect the generalist humanoid story to take 5–10 years longer than capital is currently pricing, and we expect domain-specific manufacturing automation to be the visible adoption curve in the meantime.
